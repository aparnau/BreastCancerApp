Risk factors

There are several risk factors that may increase a person's chance of developing breast cancer.

There are different kinds of risk factors - some are lifestyle-related factors, which can be modified, while other factors cannot be changed.

Risk factors: Non-modifiable

Gender:

Being a woman is a major risk factor for breast cancer. While men can also develop breast cancer, it is 100 time more common in women than in men.

Age:

The risk of breast cancer increases with age, with 2 out of 3 invasive breast cancers being found in women older than 55 years.

Genetics:

Gene defects or mutations are responsible for about 5-10% of breast cancer cases.

Risk factors: Non-modifiable

Family history:

Having a family history of breast cancer increases the risk of developing breast cancer.

Breast density:

Women with dense breasts have a higher risk of breast cancer than women with less dense breasts. Breast density is affected by factors such as age, menopausal status, the use of drugs (such as menopausal hormone therapy), pregnancy, and genetics.

Risk factors: Non-modifiable

Menstrual periods:

Being younger at the time of the first menstrual period, or starting menopause at a later age increases the risk of breast cancer.

Treatment with radiation therapy to the breast or chest:

Children or young adults who have had radiation therapy of the breast or chest are at significantly higher risk of breast cancer compared to those who have not undergone radiation therapy.

The risk varies with age and is highest if the radiation exposure was at the time of adolescence when the breasts were still developing.

Risk factors: Modifiable

Having children:

Having no children, or having the first child after the age of 30 slightly increases the risk of breast cancer.

Birth control:

Use of oral contraceptives has been found to slightly increase the risk of breast cancer.

Risk factors: Modifiable

Hormone therapy after menopause:

The use of combined hormone therapy after menopause increases the risk of breast cancer, and also the likelihood of advanced stage cancer.

Other hormone therapies have not found to contribute to higher risk.

Breastfeeding:

Breastfeeding has been found to lower breast cancer risk, especially if breastfeeding is continued for 1.5-2 years.

Alcohol:

Alcohol consumption increases the risk of breast cancer.

Risk factors: Modifiable

Overweight/obesity:

Overweight or obesity after menopause has been linked to an increased risk of breast cancer.

Physical activity:

Regular physical activity reduces the risk of breast cancer.

Recommendations for early detection

It is recommended that women in their 20s and 30s should have a clinical breast exam every 3 years.

For women 40 years and older, a clinical breast exam and a mammogram is recommended once every year.

Recommendations for early detection

An annual MRI is recommended, in addition to the annual mammogram.

It may, however, not be necessary. Discuss with your doctor if it is required.

Moderate risk women include -

Women with a lifetime risk of breast cancer of 15% to 20%, according to risk assessment tools

Women who have a personal history of breast cancer

Women who have extremely dense breasts or unevenly dense breasts when viewed by mammograms

Recommendations for early detection

An MRI and a mammogram every year is recommended.

High risk women include -

Women with a known BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutation

Women who have a first-degree relative (parent, brother, sister, or child) with a BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutation

Women having a lifetime risk of breast cancer of 20% to 25% or greater, according to risk assessment tools

Women who have had radiation therapy to the chest when they were between the ages of 10 and 30 years

Women having syndromes such as Li-Fraumeni, Cowden, or Bannayan-Riley-Ruvalcaba, or having one of these syndromes in first-degree relatives.
